Calculate Maximum Hearth Rate
- Run several 2-minute uphill runs. Get a heart-rate reading at the top of the first hill run, and if your heart rate is higher the second time up, go for a third time and see if that is associated with an even higher heart rate. If it is not higher, you can be pretty sure that reading is maximum. If the third run is higher than the second, then try a fourth, or as many as needed before you do not see an increase in heart rate compared with the previous run.
- If no hill is available, you could just do a few 800-meter runs at a solid pace and do the same comparisons between repeated efforts

# Increase Running Cadence

Cadence is individual, and the "magic" 180 SPM is more of a guideline than a rule. Many efficient, injury-free runners naturally sit anywhere from 160 to 190. That said, if you _feel_ sluggish at ~160 and worry about injury, gently nudging cadence upwards can help reduce overstriding and lower impact forces.

Here are some practical ways to work on it without forcing it too much:

---

### 1. **Awareness First**

- Just tracking your cadence during runs (many watches show it) can make you more mindful.
- Don‚Äôt try to jump straight from 160 to 180. Even adding 3‚Äì5 steps per minute can already make running feel lighter.

---

### 2. **Use Short Intervals for Practice**

- Once a week, do short segments (e.g. 1‚Äì3 minutes) where you consciously increase cadence slightly (around +5% over your natural).    
- Then return to your comfortable rhythm. Over time, the quicker turnover will ‚Äústick.‚Äù

---

### 3. **Think "Quick Feet, Light Steps"**

- Instead of trying to run _faster_, think of pulling your foot up under you quicker.
- Visual cues:
    - Pretend you‚Äôre ‚Äúrunning quietly‚Äù (reduces heavy landing).
    - Imagine the ground is hot and you need to get your foot off it quickly.

---

### 4. **Use a Metronome / Music**

- Set a metronome or running playlist a few beats above your natural cadence.
    
- Example: if you‚Äôre usually 160, try music around 165‚Äì168 SPM.
    

---

### 5. **Shorten Your Stride Slightly**

- Often, low cadence comes from overstriding.
- Focus on letting your foot land under your hips instead of far in front.
- This naturally raises cadence without extra effort.

---

### 6. **Strength & Mobility Work**

- Cadence is easier to improve if you have good hip strength and ankle mobility.
- Exercises that help:
    - Single-leg step-ups (with drive)
    - High knees (drills, not sprints)
    - Calf raises & hops
    - Glute activation (monster walks, banded clamshells)

---

### 7. **Patience & Gradual Change**

- Jumping cadence too quickly can feel awkward and even increase injury risk.
- A safe progression is **+5 SPM every few weeks** until you find a rhythm that feels smooth and less ‚Äúsluggish.‚Äù
